Foundational Logic (Prerequisite)

Basic logical vocabulary.

ConceptNotationMeaningProof Relevance
Implication \( p \to q \) "if \(p\) then \(q\)" Direct proof assumes \(p\) and derives \(q\).
Contrapositive \( \lnot q \to \lnot p \) Logically equivalent to \(p \to q\). Used in proofs by contraposition.
Contradiction \( \lnot p \to (r \land \lnot r) \) \( \lnot p \) leads to a falsehood. Used in proofs by contradiction.
Biconditional \( p \leftrightarrow q \) \( (p \to q) \land (q \to p) \) Used for equivalence proofs (two directions).
Quantifiers \( \forall x\,P(x),\ \exists x\,P(x) \) "for all", "there exists" Universal vs. existential proofs.
Negation of quantifiers \( \lnot\forall x\,P(x) \equiv \exists x\,\lnot P(x) \) Flip quantifier, negate predicate. Used in counterexamples and contradiction setups.

Study action: Write truth tables for \(p \to q\), \(\lnot q \to \lnot p\), and \(p \leftrightarrow q\). Prove that \(\lnot(p \to q) \equiv p \land \lnot q\).

Core Proof Methods (The "Big Five")

Each method has a template, a heuristic for when to use it, and typical pitfalls.

— Direct Proof

  1. Assume the hypothesis \(p\) is true.
  2. Use definitions, axioms, and earlier theorems.
  3. Deduce the conclusion \(q\) step by step.
  4. End with a clear statement such as "Therefore \(p \to q\)".

When to use: The hypothesis gives concrete algebraic or arithmetic information (parity, divisibility, set membership).

Example: If \(n\) is even, then \(n^2\) is even. Assume \(n = 2k\). Then \(n^2 = 4k^2 = 2(2k^2)\), which is even.

— Proof by Contraposition

  1. State that you will prove the contrapositive: \(\lnot q \to \lnot p\).
  2. Assume \(\lnot q\).
  3. Derive \(\lnot p\).
  4. Conclude that the original implication \(p \to q\) holds.

When to use: It is easier to start from \(\lnot q\) than from \(p\). Many parity and divisibility statements fall here.

Example: If \(n^2\) is even, then \(n\) is even. The contrapositive: if \(n = 2k+1\) is odd, then \(n^2 = 2(2k^2+2k)+1\) is odd.

— Proof by Contradiction

  1. Assume the negation: \(\lnot p\) or \(p \land \lnot q\).
  2. Derive a contradiction of the form \(r \land \lnot r\).
  3. Conclude the original statement must be true.

When to use: The statement is negative ("no largest prime"), or direct/contrapositive routes are unclear.

Example: Prove \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational. Assume \(\sqrt{2} = a/b\) in lowest terms. Then \(2b^2 = a^2\), so both \(a\) and \(b\) are even — contradicting lowest terms.

Pitfall: The contradiction must arise from your own assumptions, not from external facts.

— Proof by Cases (Exhaustion)

  1. Identify mutually exclusive, exhaustive cases \(c_1, \dots, c_k\).
  2. Prove \(c_i \to q\) for each case \(i\).
  3. Conclude that \(q\) holds in all cases.

When to use: The hypothesis naturally splits into cases (parity, modular residue, sign).

Example: For any integer \(n\), \(n^2 \equiv 0\) or \(1 \pmod{3}\). Cases \(n \equiv 0, 1, 2\) give residues \(0, 1, 4 \equiv 1\).

— Mathematical Induction (Weak and Strong)

Weak induction template:

  1. Base step: Prove \(P(n_0)\).
  2. Inductive hypothesis: Assume \(P(k)\) holds for arbitrary \(k \ge n_0\).
  3. Inductive step: Prove \(P(k) \to P(k+1)\).
  4. Conclude \(\forall n \ge n_0,\, P(n)\).

Strong induction template:

  1. Base steps: Prove \(P(a), \dots, P(b)\).
  2. Strong hypothesis: Assume \(P(a), \dots, P(k)\) all hold.
  3. Step: Prove \(P(k+1)\) using the full set of assumptions.
  4. Conclude \(\forall n \ge a,\, P(n)\).

Classic examples:

  • Sum formulas: \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\).
  • Divisibility: \(3 \mid (n^3 - n)\).
  • Inequalities: \(2^n > n^2\) for \(n \ge 5\).
  • Recurrences: \(F_n < 2^n\) for Fibonacci numbers.
  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic via strong induction.

Pitfall checklist:

  • Base case matches the stated domain.
  • Inductive step uses the inductive hypothesis explicitly.
  • Strong induction: state clearly which earlier cases are used.

Specialized Proof Types

TypeGoalTemplate Core
Existence \((\exists x\,P(x))\) At least one object works. Constructive: exhibit a concrete \(x\). Non-constructive: contradiction or external theorems.
Uniqueness \((\exists! x\,P(x))\) Existence + at most one solution. Prove existence, then assume \(x, y\) both satisfy \(P\) and show \(x = y\).
Equivalence \((p \leftrightarrow q)\) Two statements are logically equivalent. Prove \(p \to q\) and \(q \to p\) separately.
Counterexample Disprove \(\forall x\,P(x)\). Find one explicit \(x\) with \(\lnot P(x)\).
Vacuous / trivial Implications with false antecedent or always-true consequent. Note hypothesis never holds, or conclusion is always true.

Proof-Writing Hygiene

  • Declare variables ("Let \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\) …").
  • State the hypothesis explicitly ("Assume \(n\) is even.").
  • Write in complete sentences; keep verbs outside math symbols.
  • Justify each step ("by definition of even", "by the inductive hypothesis").
  • End with a recap and a closing symbol (□ or "QED").

Self-check: Did you use all hypotheses? Are definitions correct? Is the flow linear?

Classic Distributions

PMF/PDF, Expectation, and Variance for: Uniform, Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Geometric, and Normal.

Discrete Uniform Distribution

Probability Mass Function: \[ P(X=x) = \frac{1}{n} \]

\( n \)=>number of equally likely outcomes

Expectation: \[ E(X) = \frac{n+1}{2} \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \frac{n^2-1}{12} \]

Bernoulli Distribution: \( X \sim \mathrm{Ber}(p) \)

Probability Mass Function: \[ P(X=x) = \begin{cases} p & x=1 \\ 1-p & x=0 \end{cases} \]

\( p \)=>probability of success

Expectation: \[ E(X) = p \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = p(1-p) \]

Binomial Distribution: \( X \sim B(n,p) \)

Probability Mass Function: \[ P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \]

\( p \)=>probability of success per trial
\( n \)=>number of trials
\( k \)=>number of successes

Expectation: \[ E(X) = n \cdot p \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = n \cdot p(1-p) \]

Poisson Distribution: \( X \sim \mathrm{Pois}(\lambda) \)

Probability Mass Function: \[ P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} \]

\( \lambda \)=>average rate (intensity)
\( k \)=>observed count

→ Poisson Approximation

When Binomial has large \(n\) and small \(p\), use \(\lambda = np\): \[ X \approx \mathrm{Pois}(np) \]

Expectation: \[ E(X) = \lambda \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \lambda \]

Geometric Distribution: \( X \sim G(p) \)

Probability Mass Function: \[ P(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1} \cdot p \]

\( p \)=>probability of success
\( k \)=>trial number of first success

Expectation: \[ E(X) = \frac{1}{p} \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \frac{1-p}{p^2} \]

Normal Distribution: \( X \sim N(\mu,\sigma^2) \)

Probability Density Function: \[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \]

\( \mu \)=>mean (location)
\( \sigma \)=>standard deviation (spread)

→ Standard Normal

Standardize: \( Z = \dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1) \)

Expectation: \[ E(X) = \mu \]

Variance: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \sigma^2 \]

Probability Axioms

This page took this project beyond the thousand-line barrier.

Definitions and theorems for Set Theory, Probability Axioms, Bayes' theorem, chain rule, and more.

Set Theory

Union:(OR) \( A \cup B \)
Either A or B happens, or both.
Intersection:(AND) \( A \cap B \)
Both A and B happen simultaneously.
Complement:(NOT) \( A^C \) or \( \overline{A} \)
Event A doesn't happen.
Difference:(A but not B) \( A - B \)
A happens, B doesn't.

Properties:

Union: \[ A \cup (B \cup C) = (A \cup B) \cup C \] \[ A \cup B = B \cup A,\quad A \cup A = A,\quad A \cup \Omega = \Omega \] Intersection: \[ A \cap (B \cap C) = (A \cap B) \cap C \] \[ A \cap B = B \cap A,\quad A \cap A = A,\quad A \cap \Omega = A \] Complement: \( \overline{A} = \Omega - A \)
Disjoint (Mutually Exclusive): \( A \cap B = \emptyset \)
Symmetric difference: \( A \Delta B = (A-B) \cup (B-A) \)

\( \Omega \)=>Sample Space
\( \omega \)=>Sample Outcome

De Morgan's Laws:

\[ \overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cap \overline{B} \] \[ \overline{A \cap B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B} \]

Number of Elements:

\[ n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B) \] Three sets: \[ n(A \cup B \cup C) = n(A)+n(B)+n(C) \] \[ -\,n(A \cap B)-n(A \cap C)-n(B \cap C)+n(A \cap B \cap C) \] Complements: \[ n(\overline{A}) = n(\Omega) - n(A) \] \[ n(\overline{A} \cap \overline{B}) = n(\Omega) - n(A \cup B) \]

Partition:

\(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n = \Omega\) and \(A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset\) for \(i \ne j\).

Probability Axioms

1. \(P(A) \ge 0\) for all \(A \subset \Omega\)
2. \(P(\Omega) = 1\)
3. For disjoint events: \[ P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots) = \sum_i P(A_i) \]

Properties:

\(P(\emptyset) = 0\)
\(P(\overline{A}) = 1 - P(A)\)
\(0 \le P(A) \le 1\)
\(B \subseteq A \Rightarrow P(B) \le P(A)\)

\(\Omega\)=>Probability Space
\(P(\Omega)\)=>Probability Measure

Addition Rules:

\[ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \] Disjoint: \( P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) \)

Classical Probability

For Sample Space \(\Omega\): \[ P(A) = \frac{|A|}{|\Omega|} \]

\(|A|\)=>Cardinality of event A
\(|\Omega|\)=>Cardinality of Sample Space

Conditional Probability

Independence:

Two events are independent if: \[ P(A \cap B) = P(A)\,P(B) \]

Conditional probability:

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \]

For independent events: \( P(A \mid B) = P(A) \)

Chain Rule

For events \(A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n\): \[ P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^n A_i\right) = P(A_1)\,P(A_2 \mid A_1)\,P(A_3 \mid A_1 \cap A_2)\cdots \]

Law of Total Probability:

If \(A_1, \ldots, A_n\) partition \(\Omega\) and \(B\) is another event: \[ P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i)\,P(B \mid A_i) \]

Bayes' Theorem

For \(P(A), P(B) > 0\): \[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A)\,P(B \mid A)}{P(B)} \]

Extended form (partition + Bayes'):

\[ P(A_i \mid B) = \frac{P(A_i)\,P(B \mid A_i)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} P(A_j)\,P(B \mid A_j)} \]

CDF, Expectation, Variance

Definitions for: Discrete Random Variable, CDF, Expected Value, Variance, and more.

Random Variable

A random variable is a function \(X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}\) assigning each \(\omega\) a real number. \[ P(\{\omega : X(\omega) = x\}) \]

Its range is the set of values it can take: \[ \mathrm{Range}(X) = \{X(\omega) : \omega \in \Omega\} \]

Probability Mass Function

The probability of landing exactly at \(x\).

For a discrete random variable \(X\), the PMF \(p_X\) is: \[ p_X(x) = P(X = x), \quad x \in \mathrm{Range}(X) \] For any set \(A\): \( P(X \in A) = \displaystyle\sum_{x \in A} p_X(x) \)

Probability Density Function

The intensity of probability around \(x\).

A continuous random variable \(X\) satisfies: \[ P(a \le X \le b) = \int_{a}^{b} f_X(x)\,dx \] for some non-negative function \(f_X\), called the Probability Density Function.

Cumulative Distribution Function

The CDF \(F_X\) of a random variable \(X\): \[ F_X(x) = P(X \le x), \quad x \in \mathbb{R} \]

Properties:

\[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} F_X(x) = 0 \] \[ \lim_{x \to +\infty} F_X(x) = 1 \] \[ 0 \le F_X(x) \le 1 \]

For discrete \(X\): \[ F_X(x) = \sum_{y \le x} p_X(y) \] \[ P(a \le X \le b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a) \]

Continuous random variable:

\[ F_X'(x) = f_X(x) \] \[ F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\,dt \]

Expectation

For a random variable \(X\): \[ E(X) = \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} X(\omega) \cdot P(\omega) \] For PMF \(p_X\): \[ E(X) = \sum_{x \in \mathrm{Range}(X)} x \cdot p_X(x) \]

Continuous:

\[ E(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} x \cdot f(x)\,dx \]

Law of the Unconscious Statistician:

For \(Y = g(X)\): \[ E(g(X)) = \sum_x g(x)\,p_X(x) \] \[ E(g(X)) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} g(x)\,f(x)\,dx \]

Linearity:

\[ E(aX) = a\,E(X) \] \[ E(X+b) = E(X)+b \] \[ E(aX+b) = a\,E(X)+b \]

Variance

For \(E(X) = \mu\): \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2] \]

Alternative formula: \[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - \mu^2 \]

Theorem:

\[ \mathrm{Var}(aX) = a^2\,\mathrm{Var}(X) \]

Permutation && Combinatorics

Formulas for: permutation with/without repetition, identical items, and combinations.

Permutation with Repetition

All data: \[ P(n,n) = n^{n} \]

Order matters, elements can be repeated.

Selection \(r\): \[ P(n,r) = n^{r} \]

\(n\)=>all available choices
\(r\)=>selection size

Permutation without Repetition

All data: \[ P(n,n) = n! \]

Order matters, each element used at most once.

Selection \(r\): \[ P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} \]

\(n\)=>all available choices
\(r\)=>selection size

Permutation with Identical Items

All data: \[ P(n,n) = \frac{n!}{p_1!\,p_2!\cdots p_k!} \]

Order matters; identical items counted only once.

Selection \(r\): \[ P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!\,p_1!\,p_2!\cdots p_k!} \]

\(n\)=>all available choices
\(r\)=>selection size
\(p_k\)=>identical items of type \(k\)

Combination

All data: \[ C(n,n) = \binom{n}{n} = 1 \]

Order doesn't matter.

Selection \(r\): \[ C(n,r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!} \]

\(n\)=>all available choices
\(r\)=>selection size

Properties

\[ P(n,r) = C(n,r) \cdot r! \] \[ C(n,0) = \binom{n}{0} = 1 \]
